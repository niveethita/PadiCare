The Malaysian Society of Soil Science (MSSS) Newsletter Issue 1, 2020, offers an insightful and wide-ranging overview of the society’s achievements, scientific priorities, institutional resilience, and future aspirations during its 49th year of establishment. As one of Malaysia’s leading professional bodies dedicated to soil research, land management, and sustainable agriculture, MSSS continued to play an important national and international role despite the severe disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. Under the leadership of President Dr. Rosazlin Abdullah, the society demonstrated adaptability, responsibility, and long-term vision while responding to one of the most challenging global crises in recent history. Major gatherings such as SOILS2020 had to be postponed in line with Malaysia’s Movement Control Order (MCO), reflecting the society’s commitment to protecting the health and safety of members, researchers, students, and stakeholders. Although physical activities were delayed, MSSS remained active in advancing scientific communication, supporting collaborative research, and promoting awareness of soil as a vital natural resource essential for life, food production, biodiversity, and climate balance. The newsletter also reaffirmed the society’s support for the International Union of Soil Sciences (IUSS) and global sustainability efforts. Special attention was given to World Soil Day on December 5th, celebrated worldwide under the 2020 theme: “Keep soil alive, Protect soil biodiversity!” This message emphasized that healthy soils are living ecosystems filled with microorganisms, insects, fungi, roots, and nutrients that sustain agriculture and environmental health.

The pandemic also exposed weaknesses in food systems across the world, especially the vulnerability of supply chains, dependence on imports, labor shortages, and transportation disruptions. In Malaysia, these concerns intensified public discussion about national food security and agricultural self-reliance. Dr. Jeyanny Vijayanathan highlighted several lessons from the crisis, explaining that future food systems must become more resilient, localized, and environmentally sustainable. She noted that the Asia Pacific region is projected to contribute 51% of global agriculture and fish output by 2029, making the region critically important to future food supply. However, achieving this potential requires stronger planning, smarter land use, and better protection of soil resources. Among the recommended strategies were stronger support for local farmers and domestic food products, wider adoption of urban gardening and household food production, improved implementation of the National Food Security Policy, and crop-tree diversification systems that increase productivity while reducing ecological risk. Diversified farms can improve soil fertility, reduce pest outbreaks, enhance carbon storage, and create more stable incomes for farmers. Dr. Jeyanny stressed that sustainable agriculture must balance three connected goals: people, planet, and profit. This means ensuring fair livelihoods for farmers, protecting ecosystems, and maintaining economic viability. Such outcomes depend on strategic partnerships among government agencies, private companies, researchers, universities, cooperatives, and local communities.

A major highlight of the newsletter is its strong focus on practical soil management and innovation in organic agriculture. One important study, funded by the Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation (MOSTI) and carried out by the Malaysian Cocoa Board, examined the feasibility of organic cocoa cultivation in Kunak, Sabah. The research site consisted of Vertisol soil, which presents multiple challenges for agriculture. Vertisols often contain heavy clay, poor drainage characteristics, seasonal cracking, low organic matter, and acidic conditions with pH values below 5. Such soils can restrict root growth and nutrient availability if not properly managed. Over a period of twenty-one months, researchers applied organic fertilizers derived from cocoa pod husks, turning agricultural waste into a valuable soil amendment. This circular-economy approach reduced waste while improving soil quality. Results showed a slight but meaningful rise in soil pH, helping reduce acidity stress. At the same time, levels of nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium improved, supporting stronger plant nutrition. Cocoa yields were found to be comparable to those achieved under conventional inorganic fertilization systems, demonstrating that organic production can remain commercially viable. In addition, organically managed soils appeared more resilient to insect attacks and plant diseases, likely due to improved soil biology and stronger plant health. Once formally certified, the site is expected to become Malaysia’s first certified organic cocoa farm, marking a significant milestone for the nation’s premium agricultural exports and sustainable cocoa industry.

Another valuable case study featured in the newsletter involved urban agriculture in Kuala Lumpur, where researchers assessed the growth of green spinach (*Amaranthus sp.*) under different soil amendment treatments. As urban farming grows in importance due to land scarcity, rising food prices, and interest in healthy lifestyles, efficient soil management becomes essential. The study compared conventional fertilizer practices with compost and biochar applications. Compost, produced from decomposed organic materials, is known to improve soil fertility, microbial activity, and structure. Biochar, a stable carbon-rich material created through pyrolysis, has attracted global attention for its agricultural and environmental benefits. Researchers noted that biochar can increase soil pH, improve water retention, enhance cation exchange capacity (CEC), provide habitat for beneficial microbes, and store carbon for long periods. The findings showed that combining compost and biochar with standard fertilizers generated better plant growth and yields than using any one treatment alone. Soil structure became more stable, moisture-holding capacity improved, and the need for chemical fertilizer inputs was reduced. These results are especially significant for city farming, rooftop gardens, school gardens, and community plots where soils are often shallow, degraded, or low in nutrients. The research demonstrates that organic amendments can help cities produce fresh vegetables while reducing waste and environmental impact.

The newsletter also highlights methodological progress in soil science, particularly in understanding root competition and below-ground interactions in agroforestry and mixed cropping systems. Traditionally, scientists studying root systems relied on methods such as trenching, soil coring, excavation, and root washing. These techniques are labor-intensive, destructive, and often unable to distinguish roots belonging to different plant species once samples are mixed. This creates major limitations when evaluating competition for water and nutrients between trees and crops grown together. To solve this problem, newer studies increasingly use quantitative PCR (qPCR) and amplicon sequencing technologies. These molecular tools allow researchers to identify root material at the species level and estimate the abundance of different root systems within the same soil sample. This creates new opportunities to measure how roots interact, compete, or cooperate in agroforestry landscapes. Such knowledge is valuable for designing systems where fruit trees, timber trees, legumes, and annual crops can coexist efficiently. Better root science can help improve land productivity, reduce erosion, optimize fertilizer use, and create more climate-resilient farming systems across Malaysia and the wider tropics.

In the field of tropical peatland science, the newsletter presents the work of Dr. Marshall Kana Samuel on artificial peat compaction and land subsidence. Tropical peatlands are among the world’s most carbon-dense ecosystems, storing vast amounts of organic matter accumulated over thousands of years. However, when peatlands are drained for agriculture or development, the land surface often sinks. This subsidence is frequently misunderstood as direct evidence of carbon loss. Dr. Marshall introduced the “triple-C” concept to explain that peat subsidence can arise from multiple physical processes. The first component, C1, refers to compaction and shrinkage in the aerobic surface layer. The second, C2, refers to primary consolidation in deeper saturated layers. The third, C3, refers to slower secondary consolidation over time. Because peat naturally expands and contracts with changes in water table levels, and because its compressibility can reach 300% to 400%, physical lowering of the land surface does not automatically mean that peat carbon has been oxidized and released. This distinction is crucial for environmental policy, carbon accounting, plantation management, and restoration planning. Misinterpreting peat movement can lead to inaccurate emissions estimates and poor land-use decisions. The article reinforces the need for better monitoring methods that separate mechanical subsidence from biochemical decomposition.

Beyond research, the MSSS newsletter emphasizes education, youth development, and environmental stewardship. A notable initiative was the PUTRA@ECO-YOUTH program, jointly organized by Universiti Putra Malaysia Bintulu Sarawak Campus (UPMKB) and SMK Bandar Bintulu. The program successfully involved 150 young participants in environmental learning activities such as tree planting, ecological awareness sessions, and discussions on carbon footprints. These initiatives are important because future sustainability depends not only on scientific discovery but also on public understanding and youth leadership. By engaging students early, programs like this help build environmental responsibility, interest in agricultural sciences, and awareness of the relationship between soils, forests, water, and climate. The newsletter shows that MSSS views outreach as an essential part of its mission rather than a secondary activity.

The publication also celebrates professional excellence within the society. One distinguished member recognized was Dr. Wan Rasidah Binti Wan Ab Kadir, an MSSS Fellow and co-author of the book *SETIU New Forest: A Gift from Nature*. The book documents the successful rehabilitation of Beach Ridges Interspersed with Swales (BRIS) soils in Setiu, Terengganu. BRIS soils are generally sandy, nutrient-poor, drought-prone, and considered difficult for agriculture or forestry. Yet through innovative land management, careful species selection, and ecological restoration practices, the once-problematic landscape was transformed into a thriving forest ecosystem that supports valuable plant and animal life. This example challenges the assumption that marginal soils are useless. Instead, it demonstrates that scientific planning and long-term stewardship can unlock ecological and economic value even in difficult environments.

Looking ahead, MSSS encouraged members to remain globally engaged and scientifically connected. The newsletter highlighted the upcoming 22nd World Congress of Soil Science (WCSS22) in Glasgow, Scotland, as an important platform for international collaboration, knowledge exchange, and visibility for Malaysian research. Participation in global congresses allows local scientists to share tropical expertise, learn from emerging technologies, and build networks that benefit national agriculture and environmental management.

Overall, MSSS Newsletter Issue 1, 2020 reflects a dynamic and forward-looking scientific community. Despite pandemic disruptions, the society remained committed to research excellence, practical innovation, youth outreach, environmental awareness, and global cooperation. Its featured studies demonstrate how soil science directly supports food security, climate resilience, sustainable farming, and smarter land management. From organic cocoa in Sabah to urban spinach in Kuala Lumpur, from peatland mechanics to molecular root analysis, the newsletter shows that healthy soils are central to Malaysia’s future prosperity.
