The Malaysian Society of Soil Science (MSSS) Newsletter Issue 1, 2020, provides a comprehensive overview of the society's activities, pioneering research advancements, and strategic responses to global challenges during its 49th year of establishment. Under the leadership of President Dr. Rosazlin Abdullah, the society navigated the unprecedented disruptions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic, which necessitated the postponement of major events like SOILS2020 to prioritize member health and safety during Malaysia's Movement Control Order (MCO). Despite these logistical setbacks, the MSSS remained deeply committed to promoting the International Union of Soil Sciences (IUSS) initiatives and advocating for global soil awareness. The society placed a special emphasis on the UN-declared World Soil Day, celebrated on December 5th, which for 2020 carried the theme: "Keep soil alive, Protect soil biodiversity!".

The pandemic heavily underscored the vulnerability of global food supply chains, prompting a critical re-evaluation of agricultural sustainability and food security in Malaysia. Dr. Jeyanny Vijayanathan outlined essential lessons learned from the crisis, noting that the Asia Pacific region is anticipated to account for 51% of global agriculture and fish output by 2029. She advocated for revolutionary approaches to ensure stable food production, proposing key strategies such as supporting local agricultural products, adopting urban gardening for self-sufficiency, adhering to the National Food Security Policy, and implementing crop-tree diversification systems. Striking a balance between people, the planet, and profits requires smart strategic partnerships uniting the government, industry players, and local communities.

A significant portion of the newsletter is dedicated to practical soil management and innovative organic farming studies. One prominent study, funded by the Ministry of Science, Technology and Innovation (MOSTI) and conducted by the Malaysian Cocoa Board, focused on organic cocoa farming in Kunak, Sabah. The research utilized a Vertisol soil type, which is typically characterized by low organic matter, high clay content, and high acidity (pH below 5). Over twenty-one months, the application of organic fertilizers—specifically utilizing cocoa pod husks—resulted in a slight increase in soil pH and positively impacted nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium concentrations. This translated into crop yields comparable to conventional inorganic farming, significantly improving the soil's ability to withstand insect attacks and plant diseases. Once approved, this site is slated to become Malaysia's first certified organic cocoa farm.

Similarly, research conducted in an urban community garden in Kuala Lumpur explored the growth performance of green spinach (*Amaranthus sp.*) using various organic soil amendments. The study compared the use of standard fertilizers with compost (a decomposed organic material) and biochar (a carbon-rich byproduct of pyrolysis). Biochar is noted for its ability to increase soil pH, improve moisture-holding capacity, attract useful microbes, and improvise cation exchange capacity (CEC). The study demonstrated that combining compost and biochar with standard fertilizers yielded superior results compared to using either amendment alone, improving yield production, stabilizing soil structure, increasing water holding capacity, and effectively reducing the need for chemical fertilizer inputs.

Methodological advancements in soil science are also highlighted, particularly in the study of complex root interactions within agroforestry and mixed cropping systems. Historically, quantifying below-ground competition has been hindered by laborious, time-consuming methods like root washing, trenching, and soil coring, which fail to accurately distinguish mixed root samples. However, recent studies advocate for the use of quantitative PCR (qPCR) and amplicon sequencing techniques to precisely measure root interactions, offering a breakthrough for evaluating crop performance in agroforestry.

In the realm of tropical peatland studies, Dr. Marshall Kana Samuel explores the complexities of tropical peat artificial compaction. He introduces the "triple-C" concept to explain subsidence: a combination of compaction and shrinkage (C1) in the aerobic layer, alongside primary consolidation (C2) and secondary consolidation (C3) in the saturated anaerobic layer. The discourse emphasizes the need to critically differentiate between compaction caused by heavy machinery or drainage shrinkage, and actual carbon loss via oxidation. Because peat expands and contracts with water table fluctuations and possesses a compressibility level of 300% to 400%, peat compaction does not invariably equate to carbon loss.

Education and community outreach remain pivotal to the MSSS mission. The PUTRA@ECO-YOUTH program, a collaborative effort between Universiti Putra Malaysia Bintulu Sarawak Campus (UPMKB) and SMK Bandar Bintulu, successfully engaged 150 youths in activities like tree planting to educate them on carbon footprints and environmental conservation. The newsletter also honors distinguished members, featuring Dr. Wan Rasidah Binti Wan Ab Kadir, an MSSS Fellow who co-authored the book "SETIU New Forest: A Gift from Nature". This book details the successful transformation of problematic Beach Ridges Interspersed with Swales (BRIS) soil into a thriving forest ecosystem supporting valuable flora and fauna, proving that innovative thinking can make environmentally sustainable land use possible.

Looking toward the future, the MSSS encourages its members to engage globally, notably highlighting the upcoming 22nd World Congress of Soil Science (WCSS22) in Glasgow, Scotland. Overall, the newsletter reflects a vibrant scientific community dedicated to securing agricultural sustainability through advanced soil science.
