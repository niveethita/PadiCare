## Section 1: Diseases in Brassica and Leafy Vegetable Crops

### Downy Mildew in Brassica Crops (Peronospora parasitica)

Downy mildew in brassica vegetables such as cabbage (Brassica oleracea var. capitata), mustard greens (Brassica juncea), and choy sum (Brassica rapa var. parachinensis) is caused by the oomycete pathogen Peronospora parasitica. The disease is favoured by cool, moist conditions with temperatures between 10°C and 15°C and relative humidity exceeding 85%, which are commonly found in Malaysian highland growing areas such as Cameron Highlands and Kundasang in Sabah. Infected plants first display pale yellow or chlorotic lesions on the upper leaf surface, which are matched by a white to greyish-purple downy fungal growth on the corresponding lower surface. In severe infections, these lesions merge and cause extensive yellowing, browning, and premature leaf drop, significantly reducing the marketable quality of the crop. Management strategies include selecting resistant or tolerant varieties, practising crop rotation with non-host crops, maintaining adequate plant spacing to improve airflow, and removing infected plant debris immediately after harvest. Registered fungicides for chemical control include metalaxyl-M (Ridomil Gold), mancozeb, and propamocarb hydrochloride, which should be applied preventively before disease onset or at the first sign of infection.

### Club Root in Cabbage (Plasmodiophora brassicae)

Club root is a serious soil-borne disease of brassica crops caused by the obligate intracellular parasite Plasmodiophora brassicae. The pathogen produces large, club-shaped galls on the roots of affected plants, disrupting the uptake of water and nutrients and causing above-ground symptoms of wilting during the heat of the day, stunted growth, and progressive yellowing of leaves. The resting spores of Plasmodiophora brassicae are extremely persistent in the soil and can remain viable for up to 20 years, making eradication virtually impossible once a field is contaminated. The disease is favoured by acidic soil conditions with a pH below 6.5 and waterlogged or poorly drained soils. In Malaysian highland farms with continuous brassica cultivation, club root represents one of the most economically damaging soil-borne constraints. Management relies on raising soil pH to above 7.2 through the application of agricultural lime or dolomite, which inhibits spore germination and root infection. Strict crop rotation of at least four years with non-brassica crops is recommended to reduce the inoculum load in the soil. Resistant varieties developed for tropical conditions are preferred where available, and infected plant material including roots should never be composted but instead buried deeply or burned.

### Black Rot in Brassicas (Xanthomonas campestris pv. campestris)

Black rot is a bacterial disease caused by Xanthomonas campestris pv. campestris and is one of the most destructive diseases of brassica crops worldwide, including in Malaysia. The pathogen typically enters the plant through leaf margins at water pores (hydathodes) or through wounds, producing the characteristic V-shaped yellow to brown lesions pointing inward from the leaf edge. Infected vascular tissue turns black or dark brown, which is clearly visible when a cross-section of the petiole or stem is examined. The bacterium is seed-borne and can also survive in infected crop debris and on cruciferous weeds. In Malaysian conditions, the warm and humid climate accelerates disease spread, and outbreaks are commonly reported in the lowland and mid-elevation vegetable growing areas of Selangor, Perak, and Johor. Management includes the use of certified disease-free or hot-water treated seed (50°C for 30 minutes), removal and destruction of infected plant material, avoidance of overhead irrigation which promotes leaf wetness and bacterial spread, and a minimum two-year crop rotation with non-host crops. Copper-based bactericides such as copper hydroxide (Kocide) can be applied preventively but must be used judiciously to avoid phytotoxicity.

## Section 2: Diseases in Long Beans and Brinjal

### Cercospora Leaf Spot in Long Beans (Cercospora canescens)

Long beans (Vigna unguiculata subsp. sesquipedalis) are a widely cultivated vegetable crop in Malaysia and are susceptible to Cercospora leaf spot, caused by the fungal pathogen Cercospora canescens. Symptoms appear as small, circular to irregular brown spots with pale grey centres and darker brown or reddish-brown margins on the leaves. Under conditions of high humidity, a faint greyish fungal sporulation may be visible on the underside of lesions. Severe infections can lead to early defoliation, reducing the photosynthetic capacity of the plant and lowering pod yield and quality. The disease is particularly prevalent during periods of prolonged wet weather and is spread by wind-dispersed conidia and rain splash. Management includes maintaining good field sanitation, avoiding excessive nitrogen fertilisation which promotes lush growth susceptible to infection, and applying registered fungicides such as mancozeb or chlorothalonil at the first appearance of symptoms.

### Phomopsis Blight in Brinjal (Phomopsis vexans)

Brinjal (Solanum melongena), known locally as terung, is susceptible to Phomopsis blight caused by the fungus Phomopsis vexans, which can infect all aerial parts of the plant including leaves, stems, and fruits. Leaf symptoms appear as large, irregular, pale brown lesions with concentric zonation, while stem infections cause dark, sunken cankers that can girdle the stem and lead to the collapse of the affected branch. Fruit symptoms are characterised by dark, water-soaked, sunken lesions that rapidly expand and may cause complete fruit rot, rendering the produce unmarketable. The pathogen is seed-borne and can also survive on infected plant debris in the soil. In Malaysia, warm temperatures and high humidity throughout the year create persistently favourable conditions for the disease. Management practices include the use of healthy, certified seed or seed treated with thiram or captan, removal of infected plant parts and crop debris, adequate spacing to improve canopy ventilation, and timely application of copper-based fungicides or mancozeb as preventive treatments.

## Section 3: Diseases in Leafy Greens (Kangkung and Bayam)

### White Rust in Kangkung (Albugo ipomoeae-aquaticae)

Kangkung (Ipomoea aquatica), one of the most commonly consumed leafy vegetables in Malaysia, is susceptible to white rust caused by the oomycete Albugo ipomoeae-aquaticae. The disease is characterised by chalky white, blister-like pustules on the undersides of leaves, with corresponding pale yellow or chlorotic areas on the upper leaf surface. Infected stems may become distorted and hypertrophied. The pathogen produces asexual sporangia that are dispersed by wind and water splash, enabling rapid spread under the warm, humid conditions prevalent in Malaysian lowland vegetable farms. Heavy infestations can significantly reduce the marketable leaf area and overall plant vigour. Cultural control measures include avoiding dense planting, removing and destroying infected plant material, and alternating kangkung with non-host crops such as leafy brassicas. The application of copper oxychloride or mancozeb at five to seven day intervals during periods of high disease pressure has been reported to be effective in reducing disease incidence.

### Damping-Off and Root Rot in Bayam (Pythium spp. and Rhizoctonia solani)

Bayam (Amaranthus spp.) seedlings are highly susceptible to damping-off caused by soilborne pathogens including Pythium aphanidermatum and Rhizoctonia solani. Pre-emergence damping-off results in seed decay before germination, while post-emergence damping-off causes the sudden collapse of young seedlings as the stem tissue at or near the soil line becomes water-soaked and necrotic. In Malaysia, these pathogens are particularly problematic during the wet season when soils remain saturated for extended periods. Older bayam plants may also develop crown rot or root rot, causing stunted growth and premature yellowing. Prevention relies on the use of well-drained, aerated growing media, avoiding over-irrigation, and ensuring adequate spacing for good air circulation around the base of plants. Nursery beds and transplant trays can be drenched with propamocarb or a suspension of Trichoderma harzianum as a biological soil treatment before sowing to suppress soilborne pathogens.

## Section 4: Differentiating Nutrient Deficiencies from Disease Symptoms

One of the most common challenges faced by Malaysian vegetable farmers is accurately distinguishing between plant disease symptoms and symptoms caused by nutrient deficiencies, as both can produce visually similar signs such as yellowing, spotting, and leaf distortion. Correct diagnosis is critical because the management responses are entirely different: applying fungicides to a nutrient-deficient plant wastes resources and does not resolve the problem, while applying fertiliser to a diseased plant without treating the pathogen may accelerate vegetative growth that is then more susceptible to infection.

### Nitrogen Deficiency vs. Downy Mildew and Fusarium Wilt

Nitrogen deficiency causes a uniform, pale yellowing (chlorosis) that begins on the oldest, lowest leaves and progresses upward toward younger foliage as the plant remobilises nitrogen reserves. The yellowing in nitrogen deficiency is typically uniform across the entire leaf blade without distinct lesion boundaries, spots, or angular patterns. In contrast, downy mildew produces angular, water-soaked lesions bounded by leaf veins, accompanied by a fuzzy grey to purple sporulation on the underside of the leaf. Fusarium wilt produces a characteristic one-sided wilting and yellowing, and the internal vascular discolouration visible when the stem is cut cross-sectionally is a definitive diagnostic indicator that is absent in nutrient deficiency cases.

### Magnesium Deficiency vs. Spider Mite Damage and Viral Symptoms

Magnesium deficiency, which is common in Malaysian soils that are naturally acidic and leached by heavy rainfall, produces an interveinal chlorosis pattern where the leaf veins remain green while the tissue between them turns yellow. This symptom first appears on older, lower leaves. Spider mite damage produces a similar bronzing and stippling of the leaf surface, but close inspection with a hand lens will reveal the presence of tiny mites, webbing on the underside of leaves, and small puncture marks from feeding. Viral infections such as those caused by Cucumber Mosaic Virus (CMV) can produce a mosaic or mottling pattern of light and dark green or yellow patches across the leaf, often accompanied by leaf distortion, puckering, or stunting, which are not features of magnesium deficiency. A simple soil or foliar test can confirm suspected magnesium deficiency, while the presence of insect vectors or infected neighbouring plants supports a viral diagnosis.

### Calcium Deficiency vs. Bacterial and Fungal Tip Burn

Calcium deficiency in fast-growing crops such as lettuce and Chinese cabbage causes tip burn, a browning and necrosis of the leaf margins and tips, particularly on young inner leaves. This occurs because calcium is relatively immobile in the plant and requires a continuous transpiration stream for delivery to actively growing tissue. Bacterial tip burn caused by soft rot bacteria such as Erwinia carotovora may produce superficially similar marginal browning but is typically associated with a water-soaked, slimy, and foul-smelling decay that spreads inward from the leaf edge. Fungal tip burn caused by Botrytis cinerea under conditions of poor ventilation produces a characteristic grey fuzzy sporulation over the necrotic tissue, which clearly distinguishes it from the dry, papery browning of calcium deficiency.

### Iron Deficiency vs. Powdery Mildew and Mosaic Virus

Iron deficiency in vegetables grown in calcareous or overly limed soils produces a pronounced interveinal chlorosis on young, newly emerging leaves at the growing tip, with the youngest leaves affected most severely while older leaves remain green. This pattern is the reverse of nitrogen or magnesium deficiency. Powdery mildew produces white, powdery fungal patches on the leaf surface that can be physically rubbed off, a feature entirely absent in nutrient deficiency. Mosaic virus produces irregular green and yellow mottling with leaf deformation, and is always associated with aphid or other vector activity in the crop history. Iron deficiency can be confirmed by applying a foliar iron chelate solution; if the new leaves recover their green colour within one to two weeks, a nutrient deficiency was the cause.

## Section 5: Seasonal and Regional Disease Risk in Malaysia

### Highland Growing Areas (Cameron Highlands, Kundasang, Genting Highlands)

Malaysian highland vegetable production, concentrated in areas such as Cameron Highlands in Pahang, Kundasang in Sabah, and the Genting Highlands foothills, operates in a cooler and wetter climate with temperatures typically ranging between 15°C and 25°C. These conditions are particularly conducive to downy mildew on brassica crops, grey mould (Botrytis cinerea) on lettuce and strawberries, and late blight (Phytophthora infestans) on tomatoes grown at altitude. The frequent mist and cloud cover in highland areas means that leaf wetness duration is prolonged, often exceeding 10 to 12 hours per day during the northeast monsoon season from November to March, creating persistently high-risk conditions for foliar pathogens. Farmers in these regions are advised to prioritise preventive fungicide programmes, use drip irrigation instead of overhead sprinklers, and install rain shelters or net houses to reduce direct leaf wetness from rainfall.

### Lowland Growing Areas (Selangor, Johor, Kedah, Kelantan)

Lowland vegetable production in Malaysia, which accounts for the majority of cucurbit, tomato, chilli, and leafy vegetable cultivation, operates under consistently high temperatures of 28°C to 35°C and high relative humidity exceeding 80% for much of the year. These conditions are most favourable for bacterial diseases such as Ralstonia solanacearum wilt and Erwinia soft rot, soilborne fungal diseases including Fusarium oxysporum wilt and Sclerotium rolfsii southern blight, and insect-vectored viral diseases such as Tomato Yellow Leaf Curl Virus (TYLCV) spread by whiteflies and Chilli Veinal Mottle Virus (ChiVMV) spread by aphids. The inter-monsoon periods in April and October, characterised by intense rainfall and short dry spells, often trigger sudden disease outbreaks due to the combination of warm temperatures, soil waterlogging, and physical wounding of plant tissue from heavy rain splash. Farmers in lowland areas should prioritise soil drainage improvements, use raised beds, and implement regular whitefly and aphid monitoring to provide early warning of viral disease risk.

### Monsoon Season Disease Management Calendar

During the southwest monsoon period from May to September, which brings drier conditions to Peninsular Malaysia west coast states including Selangor, Perak, and Kedah, fungal foliar diseases such as powdery mildew tend to increase due to the combination of warm temperatures and lower but still significant humidity. During the northeast monsoon from November to March, which brings heavy rainfall to east coast states such as Kelantan, Terengganu, and Pahang, bacterial and oomycete diseases such as downy mildew and bacterial soft rot are more prevalent due to prolonged periods of leaf wetness and soil saturation. Year-round monitoring programmes using disease forecasting tools developed by MARDI, combined with Growing Degree Day (GDD) models and real-time humidity data from farm weather stations, enable more precise timing of preventive spray applications compared to calendar-based approaches.

## Section 6: MARDI-Developed Resistant Varieties

The Malaysian Agricultural Research and Development Institute (MARDI) has developed and released a number of vegetable varieties with improved resistance or tolerance to major local diseases as part of the national effort to reduce dependence on chemical pesticides and improve farm profitability.

For tomatoes, MARDI Tomato MAHA 18 has demonstrated a survival rate exceeding 93% in soils heavily infested with Ralstonia solanacearum, and is recommended for lowland cultivation in areas with a history of bacterial wilt. Tomato MAHA 1 and MAHA 2 are earlier release varieties with moderate tolerance to Fusarium oxysporum f.sp. lycopersici race 1. For chillies, MARDI Chilli Kulai 3 and Kulai 4 have shown improved field tolerance to Colletotrichum capsici anthracnose, which is the leading cause of post-harvest fruit losses in chilli production across Malaysia. For cucumbers, several experimental lines maintained at MARDI Serdang research station have been tested for powdery mildew resistance conferred through grafting onto Cucurbita moschata rootstock variety GP8, though the use of grafted transplants rather than varietal seed resistance remains the primary deployment strategy for cucumbers. For brassica crops, several F1 hybrid cabbages and kailan (Chinese kale) varieties commercially available in Malaysia, including those distributed under the East-West Seed and Bejo Zaden brands, incorporate downy mildew resistance sourced from breeding programmes conducted in humid tropical environments.

## Section 7: Diagnostic Flowchart for Common Vegetable Diseases

### Step 1: Identify the Affected Plant Part

When diagnosing a disease problem, the first step is to identify which plant part is most severely affected. If the primary symptoms are on the leaves, proceed to assess the pattern of yellowing, spotting, or lesion characteristics. If the stem base or crown is blackened or water-soaked, consider soilborne pathogens such as Pythium, Rhizoctonia, or Sclerotium. If the entire plant is wilting despite adequate soil moisture, suspect a vascular or root pathogen such as Fusarium oxysporum or Ralstonia solanacearum. If symptoms are primarily on the fruit, consider fungal pathogens such as Colletotrichum (anthracnose), Botrytis (grey mould), or bacterial soft rot (Erwinia spp.).

### Step 2: Assess the Pattern and Distribution of Symptoms

Uniform yellowing from the bottom of the plant upward suggests nitrogen deficiency or a root problem limiting nutrient uptake. Angular lesions bounded by leaf veins are characteristic of bacterial diseases or downy mildew, where the pathogen is confined by vascular tissue. Circular lesions with distinct margins and concentric rings are more typical of fungal pathogens such as Alternaria, Cercospora, or Colletotrichum. White powdery patches that can be physically rubbed off the leaf surface are diagnostic for powdery mildew. A mosaic or mottled pattern of light and dark green without distinct lesion edges is characteristic of viral diseases.

### Step 3: Check for Pathogen Structures

Examining suspected lesions with a hand lens (10x magnification) can reveal important diagnostic features. Grey to brown sporulation on the underside of lesions indicates downy mildew or Botrytis. White cottony mycelium on the leaf surface indicates powdery mildew. Dark, pinpoint-sized fruiting bodies (pycnidia or acervuli) embedded in the lesion tissue indicate fungal pathogens such as Phoma, Phomopsis, or Colletotrichum. Orange to salmon-coloured spore masses on wet weather indicate Colletotrichum acervuli. A clear bacterial streaming test, performed by cutting a wilted stem and immersing the cut end in clear water, confirms bacterial wilt (Ralstonia solanacearum) if a milky, white ooze streams from the vascular tissue within 30 to 60 seconds.

### Step 4: Confirm Environmental and Management History

Disease diagnosis should always be contextualised with information about recent weather conditions, irrigation practices, fertilisation history, and previous crop history in the same field. Soilborne diseases are more likely in fields with a history of the same crop or related crops in previous seasons. Bacterial diseases are more common after periods of heavy rain, mechanical wounding, or insect feeding damage. Viral diseases are more common during periods of high vector activity, particularly in the dry season when whitefly and aphid populations peak. Nutrient deficiencies are more likely in recently established fields with poor organic matter content, in soils with extreme pH, or following periods of heavy leaching rainfall.

## Section 8: Fungicide Application Rates and Practical Guidance

### Recommended Application Rates for Common Registered Fungicides

All pesticide use in Malaysia must comply with the Pesticide Act 1974, and only products registered with the Pesticide Board of Malaysia under Jabatan Pertanian are legally permitted for use on food crops. The following application rates are general guidance based on registered product labels, and farmers must always refer to the specific label of the product they are using for legally compliant rates and safety instructions.

Mancozeb 80% WP (wettable powder), a multi-site protectant fungicide in FRAC group M3, is typically applied at a rate of 2.0 to 2.5 grams per litre of water for cucurbits and brassica crops, with applications at seven to ten day intervals during periods of high disease risk. The pre-harvest interval (PHI) for mancozeb on cucurbits is three days, meaning no application should be made within three days of harvest. Chlorothalonil 75% WP, another multi-site protectant in FRAC group M5, is applied at 1.5 to 2.0 grams per litre of water, with a PHI of seven days for cucurbit crops. Azoxystrobin 25% SC (suspension concentrate), a systemic strobilurin fungicide in FRAC group 11, is applied at 0.5 to 1.0 millilitre per litre of water and should not be applied more than twice consecutively due to the elevated risk of resistance development in Podosphaera xanthii. Difenoconazole 25% EC (emulsifiable concentrate), a DMI fungicide in FRAC group 3, is applied at 0.5 millilitre per litre of water. Tebuconazole, also in FRAC group 3, has a PHI of seven days on cucurbit crops. When mixing fungicides in a tank, always add wettable powders to the tank first with agitation, followed by suspension concentrates, and finally emulsifiable concentrates, to ensure uniform mixing and avoid incompatibility.

### Spray Volume and Equipment Calibration

Effective disease control depends not only on the correct fungicide and rate but also on achieving adequate spray coverage of the target plant tissue. For field-grown vegetables in Malaysia, a spray volume of 200 to 400 litres per hectare is typically recommended using conventional hydraulic knapsack or motorised sprayers, which ensures thorough coverage of both upper and lower leaf surfaces. Calibration of sprayer output should be performed at the beginning of each season and after any maintenance by collecting the spray output from each nozzle over a timed period and comparing it to the manufacturer's specification. Nozzles should be replaced when their output deviates by more than 10% from the specification, as worn nozzles produce larger droplets that reduce coverage uniformity. Spraying should be conducted in the early morning or late afternoon when wind speeds are low and temperatures are below 32°C to minimise evaporative losses and reduce the risk of phytotoxicity on heat-stressed plants.

## Section 9: Integrated Pest and Disease Management (IPDM) for Key Crops

### IPDM Programme for Cucumber Production

An integrated approach to cucumber disease management in Malaysia begins with site preparation, including soil testing to ensure pH is between 6.0 and 6.8, incorporation of well-composted organic matter to improve soil structure and biological activity, and fumigation or solarisation of fields with a history of soilborne disease. At the transplanting stage, the use of disease-free grafted seedlings on Cucurbita moschata rootstock GP8 provides systemic protection against powdery mildew and Fusarium wilt. During the vegetative and flowering stages, weekly monitoring for the first signs of powdery mildew, downy mildew, or thrips and whitefly activity should be conducted, with biological control agents such as Bacillus subtilis KB-401 applied as foliar sprays at seven to ten day intervals as a preventive measure. If disease incidence exceeds a threshold of 5% of plants showing symptoms, a single application of a registered fungicide from FRAC group 3 or 11, tank-mixed with a multi-site protectant, can be applied, followed by a return to biological controls in subsequent sprays to minimise selection pressure for resistance.

### IPDM Programme for Chilli Production

Chilli (Capsicum annuum) production in Malaysia faces pressure from a wide range of diseases including Colletotrichum anthracnose, Phytophthora blight, Cucumber Mosaic Virus (CMV), Chilli Veinal Mottle Virus (ChiVMV), and bacterial wilt. An IPDM programme for chilli begins with the selection of MARDI-recommended tolerant varieties, use of certified or treated seed, and nursery management practices that prevent early infection. Reflective silver plastic mulch should be installed before transplanting to repel winged aphids and whiteflies, which are the primary vectors of CMV and ChiVMV, and has been shown to delay the onset of viral disease by three to four weeks compared to bare soil. Yellow sticky traps placed at crop canopy height at a density of 20 to 25 traps per hectare provide ongoing monitoring of whitefly and aphid populations and indicate when vector pressure is rising. Fungicide applications for Colletotrichum should be timed to the flowering and early fruit development stages, when the pathogen has greatest access to fruit tissue through open flowers, and should use FRAC group 11 or FRAC group 3 materials rotated with copper-based protectants.

## Section 10: Post-Harvest Disease Management

### Botrytis Grey Mould in Tomatoes and Strawberries (Botrytis cinerea)

Botrytis cinerea, commonly known as grey mould, is one of the most economically important post-harvest pathogens affecting tomatoes, strawberries, and leafy vegetables in Malaysia, particularly those grown in highland conditions or stored in high-humidity environments. The fungus infects fruit and vegetable tissue at harvest wounds, stem scars, and calyx attachment points, producing the characteristic dense grey sporulation that gives the disease its common name. Under Malaysian ambient storage conditions without refrigeration, Botrytis can cause complete fruit collapse within two to three days of harvest. Post-harvest management requires careful harvesting practices that minimise physical wounding, prompt removal of field heat using forced air cooling to below 10°C, and maintenance of a cold chain at 8°C to 12°C for tomatoes and 0°C to 2°C for strawberries. Hot water dipping of tomato fruit at 52°C for two minutes immediately after harvest has been demonstrated to significantly reduce Botrytis infection rates without causing heat damage to the fruit.

### Stem-End Rot in Cucumbers and Tropical Fruits (Lasiodiplodia theobromae)

Lasiodiplodia theobromae is a common post-harvest pathogen in tropical Malaysia that causes stem-end rot in cucumbers, papayas, mangoes, and bananas. The fungus colonises the stem-end wound left after harvest and progresses inward, causing a dark, water-soaked decay that can reach the seed cavity of cucumbers within four to five days at ambient temperatures. The pathogen is ubiquitous in the Malaysian environment and is present on most farm surfaces, packaging materials, and handling equipment. Post-harvest control relies on minimising the time between harvest and refrigerated storage, sanitising all handling surfaces and containers with sodium hypochlorite solution at 100 to 200 parts per million, and applying food-grade wax coatings to the stem-end of cucumbers to reduce moisture loss and fungal entry. Research conducted by MARDI has demonstrated that rhamnolipid biosurfactants derived from Pseudomonas aeruginosa, when applied as a post-harvest dip at concentrations of 0.1% to 0.5%, can reduce Lasiodiplodia stem-end rot incidence in cucumbers by up to 85% compared to untreated controls while meeting food safety requirements.

### Soft Rot in Leafy Vegetables (Erwinia carotovora subsp. carotovora)

Bacterial soft rot caused by Erwinia carotovora subsp. carotovora (now reclassified as Pectobacterium carotovorum) is the leading cause of post-harvest losses in leafy vegetables including Chinese cabbage, lettuce, kangkung, and bayam during distribution and retail in Malaysia. The bacterium produces pectinase enzymes that break down the middle lamella of plant cell walls, causing the characteristic water-soaked, slimy, and foul-smelling decay. The bacteria enter through harvest wounds, insect feeding damage, or mechanical injuries during handling and are spread rapidly by water and contaminated surfaces. Post-harvest management focuses on the entire supply chain: harvesting in the cool morning hours to reduce field heat, removing outer leaves that are most contaminated with soil bacteria before packing, using clean and sanitised plastic crates rather than bamboo baskets that harbour bacteria, and maintaining continuous cold chain storage between 4°C and 8°C from the packinghouse through to the retail point.
